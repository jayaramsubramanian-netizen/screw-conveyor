VECTRIX™ does not merely flag when a design parameter exceeds a limit. It simulates the causal chain from input conditions to specific physical failure modes — enabling the engineer to understand not just that a design will fail, but where, how, and what changing which variable most efficiently fixes it.

Failure Mode 1
🚫 Trough Choking

§ 4.5  Computation Architecture

Solver Architecture Overview

VECTRIX™ is not spreadsheet logic. The calculation engine uses a structured solver architecture with three distinct computation patterns:

Pattern 1
Deterministic Solver
  • Single-pass direct calculation
  • No iteration required
  • Used for capacity, fill, torque, L10
  • Executes on every keystroke — always live
Pattern 2
Convergence Loops
  • Iterative constraint satisfaction
  • Used for motor/gearbox selection, shaft sizing, NTU method
  • Converges within 3–8 iterations for standard cases
  • Flagged if convergence fails
Pattern 3

  • Numerical ODE solver — transient dynamics only
  • Δt-stepped through startup ramp to steady state
  • Used for N(t), T(t), φ(t) transient profiles

§ 4.9  Instructive Bad Input Examples

What Failure Looks Like — Three Instructive Cases

Case 1 — Overfilled Screw
Scenario: Engineer selects standard pitch, 45% fill, 25° inclination
  • What happens: calcFill() applies the inclination correction f(25°) ≈ 0.55 → actual φ = 45% × 0.55 = 24.75%. But material cohesion adds a 0.15 penalty → φ_effective = 22%. Capacity drops 51% below the target.
  • Advisory fires: ⚠️ Actual fill fraction significantly below fill_max — consider increasing N or reducing inclination.
  • Fix: Increase N by 35% to recover capacity, or switch to short pitch (0.67D) which improves fill retention on inclines.
Case 2 — Wrong Density (Warm vs. Cold Material)
Scenario: Motor sized using nominal ρ = 0.72 t/m³ (dry, warm coal)
  • What happens: On a winter morning cold start, material arrives at ρ = 0.88 t/m³ (compacted, frozen surface). Running power increases by 22%; startup torque with cohesion penalty increases by 85%. Motor torque at 60% speed on DOL starter = 145% of rated — insufficient margin.
  • Advisory fires: ❌ Startup torque ratio T_start/T_run exceeds 3.5 — direct-on-line starter inadequate. VFD or soft-starter required.
  • Fix: Always run the design at ρ_max. In this case: upgrade to soft-starter, or upsize motor by one frame size.
Case 3 — High Inclination, Near Critical Speed
Scenario: 22° inclined conveyor, L = 18 m, D = 400 mm, N = 80 RPM
  • What happens: Critical speed Nc = 96 RPM for this shaft span. Operating at 80 RPM = 83% of Nc — within the 20% exclusion zone. Additionally, inclination correction drops fill by 40%, so N must be increased to 110 RPM to meet capacity — now 115% of Nc. Resonance zone is unavoidable without geometry change.
  • Advisory fires: ❌ Operating speed within 20% of critical speed Nc. Risk of lateral shaft whirl. Add hanger bearing to reduce effective span.
  • Fix: Add one hanger bearing at L/2 = 9 m. This raises Nc to 194 RPM. All issues resolved without changing shaft diameter or speed.

§ 3  Process Equipment

Process Equipment

Process modules extend the conveying backbone with thermodynamic, kinetic, and mixing physics. Each module produces process-specific outputs alongside the standard mechanical and power outputs.

🌀 Screw Mixer Process

Used for blending two or more bulk materials, or for homogenizing a single product. The VECTRIX™ mixer module implements the Lacey mixing index combined with the Axial Dispersion Model (ADM) to account for the non-ideal plug-flow behavior of screw mixers. Power consumption is modeled using the Newton number approach (Harnby, Edwards & Nienow) with flow regime classification — Sliding, Rolling, Cascading, or Centrifugal — based on the Froude number. The module supports single-shaft ribbon, paddle, and plough configurations, and twin-shaft co-rotating and counter-rotating geometries. Residence time distribution correction is applied to reflect the real mixing efficiency vs. ideal plug-flow assumptions.

🌡️ Screw Dryer Process

Screw dryers use a jacketed or hollow-flight screw to transfer heat into wet bulk material while conveying it. VECTRIX™ applies the LMTD heat transfer method (Kern, 1950) with an effective overall heat transfer coefficient corrected for fouling resistance and moving-bed contact inefficiency. Drying kinetics are modeled in two regimes: the constant-rate period (surface evaporation dominated, inlet moisture above 20%) and the falling-rate period (internal diffusion limited, below 20%). The drying duty is bounded by both the heat transfer capacity and the mass transfer rate, using a min-rule to identify the governing mechanism. Jacket temperature, steam or hot water medium, and residence time are all design variables.

❄️ Screw Cooler Process

Coolers use the same jacketed or hollow-flight construction as dryers but operate with a cooling medium (water or refrigerant) to extract heat from hot bulk material. VECTRIX™ applies the NTU–Effectiveness method (Kays & London) to model indirect heat transfer between the material bed and the cooling medium. A moving-bed correction factor (0.6–0.85) is applied to account for the reduced contact area and thermal resistance compared to a stationary packed bed. Outlet temperature, heat removal duty, and NTU are calculated for the specified residence time, material heat capacity, and cooling medium flow rate.

⚗️ Screw Reactor Process

Screw reactors provide a controlled residence time environment for solid-phase or solid-gas chemical reactions — calcination, torrefaction, pyrolysis, and catalytic processes. VECTRIX™ applies first-order reaction kinetics via the Damköhler number (Da = k·t_res), combined with the Axial Dispersion Model to correct for the non-plug-flow RTD of a screw. A Peclet number (Pe = v_ax·L/D_ax) characterizes the axial mixing level; at Pe < 5, the reactor behaves CSTR-like and plug-flow conversion assumptions are strongly non-conservative. The module uses a slip factor (S = 0.6–0.95) to account for material back-mixing relative to the theoretical screw pitch velocity.

🧱 Screw Compactor Process

Compactors use a converging screw geometry — typically a reducing pitch or taper — to progressively compress bulk material against a restricted outlet. This increases bulk density and often forms a material plug that acts as a dynamic seal. VECTRIX™ applies a Janssen-style powder compaction model to calculate the back-pressure generated by wall friction, the compression ratio (CR = ρ_target/ρ_loose), and the resulting torque demand. The module flags CR > 2.5 as a high plugging risk and quantifies the exit pressure, which is critical for screw seal design in reactors and pressurized systems.

§ 6  Resistance Factor (λ)

Resistance Factor (λ)

The resistance factor λ is the dimensionless material-specific coefficient that quantifies the total resistance a bulk solid presents to forward movement inside the screw trough. It combines particle friction, material-to-screw surface interaction, cohesive resistance, and moisture effects into a single working parameter used in the power equation.

🔗
Role in Power Equationλ appears directly in the material power term: P_m = (Q · ρ · L · g · λ) / (3600 · η). A higher λ means the material is harder to push — it increases power demand proportionally. Incorrect λ values are the most common source of under-designed conveyor drives.

Blended Calculation Model

3.2 — Slip Factor Application (D-04 Final)

The axial velocity used for capacity calculations is based on ideal screw transport. However, bulk materials do not move at the full theoretical velocity due to slip, recirculation, and back-mixing. A slip factor S is therefore applied when computing residence time for process modules.

vaxial=P · N(for capacity Qt — ideal)
tres=L / (P · N · S)(for process modules — slip-corrected)

§ 4  Critical Speed

Every rotating shaft has a critical speed — the rotational frequency at which it enters lateral resonance. At this speed, small deflections are amplified rapidly, causing severe vibration and potential shaft fracture. The operating speed must maintain a safe margin below this limit.

Material Fill Dynamics

Material does not instantaneously reach its target fill fraction when the screw starts. Feed enters through the inlet, travels axially, and progressively fills the trough from the inlet toward the outlet. This process is modeled as a first-order exponential fill-up.

§ 9  Transient Failure Modes

Transient Failure Modes

Transient failures are distinct from steady-state overloads. They occur in fractions of a second and leave no time for protective relays to respond unless pre-configured with appropriate trip delays.

💥Torque Overload

T_startup > T_gearbox rated or T_startup > T_shaft_allow. Most common transient failure. Shaft twists or gearbox output stage fractures.

Trigger: f_startup underestimated; cold/wet material; longer-than-expected lay-up period.

⏹Motor Stall

T_motor(ω) < T_load(ω) at some speed. Motor locks at that speed, draws locked-rotor current (5–8× rated) and overheats within seconds.

Trigger: DOL start on cohesive material; star-delta transition torque dip at changeover speed.

🔥Thermal Overload

Prolonged elevated current during slow acceleration overheats motor windings. Motor thermistor trips before damage — but repeated events degrade insulation progressively.

Trigger: High inertia + low T_net margin → long acceleration time; more than 3–4 starts/hour without cooling interval.

🚫Choke on Startup

At low speed, axial velocity is insufficient to clear the fill zone faster than material is fed. Fill exceeds φ_max → choking. Load spikes sharply at low ω where T_motor is also near its minimum (near sync speed trough).

Trigger: Feed not shut off before restart; high φ_target material with steep inclination.

§ 3  Drying Regimes

Drying Regimes — Constant Rate vs Falling Rate

Drying does not proceed at a uniform rate throughout the process. Two physically distinct regimes govern drying, separated by a critical moisture content X_critical. Understanding which regime governs is essential for correctly sizing the required residence time.

💧Constant Rate PeriodX > X_crit

The material surface is covered with free moisture. Evaporation occurs from a continuous liquid film at the surface. The drying rate is controlled by heat transfer from the jacket to the surface — not by diffusion within the solid. As long as free moisture is available, the drying rate remains constant and predictable. This is the fastest drying stage and is highly amenable to heat transfer enhancement.

Surface evaporationHeat transfer limitedPredictable rateU_eff governs
🌾Falling Rate PeriodX < X_crit

Free surface moisture is exhausted. Remaining moisture is bound within the particle structure — in capillaries, pores, and at hydrophilic surfaces. Water must diffuse from the interior to the surface before it can evaporate. This internal diffusion is slow and strongly material-dependent. The drying rate falls progressively as X decreases toward the equilibrium moisture content X_eq. Increasing jacket temperature has diminishing returns in this regime.

Internal diffusionMass transfer limitedTemperature-sensitiveEquilibrium approach
3.1 — Critical Moisture Content (Transition Point)
Xcritical≈0.15 – 0.25 kg/kg dry   (material-dependent; typically 15–25% wet basis)
Coarse, porous materialsX_crit ≈ 0.15 (coal, sand, gravel) — surface moisture depletes quickly
Fine powders, claysX_crit ≈ 0.25–0.40 (kaolin, bentonite, filter cake) — high specific surface holds more free moisture
Engineering implicationIf X_in > X_crit and X_target < X_crit, the dryer must handle BOTH regimes. The total required residence time is the sum of t_constant_rate + t_falling_rate.
Regime Identification in VECTRIX™: The model automatically identifies which regime(s) apply based on X_in, X_target, and X_critical. If the target is entirely within the constant-rate regime, the design is heat-transfer-dominated and is straightforward to optimize by increasing U_eff or jacket area. If the target enters the falling-rate regime, the model switches to the diffusion-limited sub-model and the dominant design lever becomes residence time (screw length and speed), not heat input.
§ 4  Heat Transfer Model

Heat Transfer Model — LMTD Method

Heat is transferred from the heating medium (steam or hot water in the jacket) to the bulk material through the screw wall and trough wall. The LMTD (Log Mean Temperature Difference) method, standard for shell-and-tube and jacketed equipment, is applied here to the moving bed in the screw annulus.

4.1 — Heat Transfer Rate
Q=Ueff·A·ΔTlm
QHeat transfer rate [W or kW]
UeffEffective overall heat transfer coefficient [W/m²K] — see §4.3
AHeat transfer area [m²] = π · D · L for a cylindrical jacket (full wrap)
ΔTlmLog mean temperature difference [°C] — see §4.2
4.2 — Log Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD)
ΔTlm=[(Th − Tout) − (Th − Tin)] / ln[(Th − Tout) / (Th − Tin)]
ThHeating medium temperature [°C] — steam saturation temperature or hot water supply temperature
TinMaterial inlet temperature [°C]
ToutMaterial outlet temperature [°C] — typically the wet-bulb temperature during constant-rate drying; the dry-bulb temperature on approach to equilibrium
ConstraintΔT_lm ≥ 15°C required for effective heat transfer. Below this, the driving force is insufficient to maintain the design drying rate — VECTRIX™ issues a ⚠️ Advisory.
⚠️
LMTD Special Case — Isothermal Heating MediumWhen the heating medium is condensing steam (approximately isothermal), T_h is constant throughout the jacket length. This simplifies the LMTD calculation — the LMTD represents the true driving force without needing a correction factor. For hot water systems where T_h drops along the jacket, an F-factor correction (typically 0.85–0.95) should be applied.
4.3 — Effective Heat Transfer Coefficient
Ueff=1 / (1/hsolid + Rfouling + 1/hwall)
Ueff≈Unominal · (1 − ffouling)
hsolidSolid-side convective coefficient [W/m²K] — strongly material and fill-dependent. Powder beds: 20–60 W/m²K. Granular: 40–100 W/m²K.
RfoulingFouling resistance [m²K/W] — typical value 0.0002 for indirect steam heating (equivalent to ~5% U reduction)
hwallWall-side (jacket) coefficient [W/m²K] — condensing steam: 6,000–15,000 W/m²K (negligible resistance); hot water: 1,000–4,000 W/m²K
ffoulingFouling penalty fraction — 0.05–0.15 for typical bulk solids. Higher for sticky or high-fat materials.
⚠️
Empirical Diameter Correction (D-09 Final — Retained) VECTRIX™ applies an empirical correction to Ueff to account for scale effects as screw diameter changes:

U_eff_actual = U_eff_ref × (0.3 / D)0.3

This reflects the reduction in specific heat transfer area (surface-to-volume ratio) as screw diameter increases. The exponent 0.3 is empirically derived; the reference diameter is 0.3 m. This correction is retained and explicitly labelled as empirical. It should be validated against plant data where heat transfer measurements for the specific screw diameter are available.

§ 5  Mass Transfer Model

Mass Transfer — Evaporation Kinetics

The mass transfer model governs the rate at which moisture migrates from the bulk solid to the surrounding gas phase. In the constant-rate period, this is straightforward. In the falling-rate period, internal diffusion resistance becomes the dominant barrier.

5.1 — Constant Rate Period — Evaporation Rate
ṁevap=kd · A · (Y* − Y)
kdMass transfer coefficient [kg/m²s] — depends on gas velocity over material surface and turbulence level inside the screw
Y*Saturation humidity [kg water/kg dry gas] at the surface temperature — from psychrometric data
YBulk gas humidity [kg water/kg dry gas] — increases along the screw length as moisture is evaporated
Y* − YHumidity driving force — decreases along the length. When Y → Y*, evaporation ceases. For indirect jacketed screws with no forced gas flow, mass transfer is typically not the limiting factor in the constant-rate regime.
5.2 — Falling Rate Period — Effective Drying Rate
ṁevap,fall=ṁevap,const · (X − Xeq) / (Xcritical − Xeq)
XeqEquilibrium moisture content — the irreducible moisture level for this material at the operating temperature and humidity. Further drying below X_eq is thermodynamically impossible without changing conditions.
Linear fallThis assumes a linear falling rate — a good first approximation. More rigorous models (exponential decay, sorption isotherm) are available for pharmaceutical and food-grade applications.
5.3 — Governing Drying Rate (Dual Limit Rule)
ṁdrying=min(ṁheat limit,   ṁmass limit)
ṁheat limit= Q_available / λ_w — how much water the available heat can evaporate per second
ṁmass limit= k_d · A · (Y* − Y) — how fast moisture can physically migrate to the evaporation surface
§ 6  Thermal Energy Balance

Thermal Energy Balance

The total heat duty of the dryer has two components: sensible heat to raise the material temperature from T_in to T_out, and latent heat to evaporate the moisture. For high-moisture feeds, the latent heat component typically dominates — often accounting for 70–90% of the total heat duty.

6.1 — Latent Heat of Water Vaporization (Temperature-Dependent)
λw=(2501 − 2.42 · T) · 1000  [J/kg]
TEvaporation surface temperature [°C]. At 20°C: λ_w = 2,453 kJ/kg. At 100°C: λ_w = 2,257 kJ/kg. At 150°C: λ_w = 2,114 kJ/kg.
SourceAntoine equation linearization — accurate to ±0.3% over 0–100°C. VECTRIX™ evaluates λ_w at the estimated surface temperature of the material, not the jacket temperature.
6.2 — Total Heat Duty
Qtotal=ṁwater · λw+ṁdry · Cp · (Tout − Tin)
ṁwater · λwLatent heat component — evaporation of the removed water. Always positive.
ṁdry · Cp · ΔTSensible heat component — heating the solid from T_in to T_out. Can be minimized by minimizing the temperature rise (drying at lower temperature with longer residence time).
CpSolid specific heat capacity [J/kg·K] — from material database. Typical ranges: minerals 700–900 J/kg·K; biomass 1200–1800 J/kg·K; food/starch 1500–2500 J/kg·K.
6.3 — Steam Consumption (if steam heating)
ṁsteam=Qtotal / λsteam
λsteamSteam latent heat at the supply pressure [kJ/kg]. At 1 barg: 2,197 kJ/kg. At 3 barg: 2,133 kJ/kg. At 6 barg: 2,067 kJ/kg.
EfficiencyDrying efficiency η_dry = Q_useful / Q_steam_input. Typical indirect screw dryers: η_dry = 0.65–0.80. Heat losses through insulation reduce this figure; longer, better-insulated machines improve it.
§ 7  Residence Time

Residence Time — Slip-Corrected Model

Residence time is the duration of material exposure to the heating surface. It is the primary design lever for the falling-rate drying regime — when heat transfer is adequate but diffusion is slow, the only way to achieve the target moisture is to keep the material in the dryer longer.

7.1 — Ideal (No Slip) Residence Time
tres,ideal=L / (P · N)
LScrew length [m]
P · NAxial transport velocity [m/s] = pitch × rev/s
7.2 — Slip-Corrected Residence Time (VECTRIX™ Model)
tres=L / (P · N · S)
SSlip factor [dimensionless] — fraction of the theoretical axial velocity actually achieved by the material. Range: 0.60–0.95.
S = 0.60–0.70High-fill, cohesive, or inclined dryers — material rotates partially with the screw rather than translating
S = 0.80–0.90Standard dry granular material in U-trough at moderate fill and inclination

Engineering effectA slip factor of 0.75 means the material takes 33% longer to traverse the screw than the theoretical ideal — which is 33% more residence time, which is beneficial for drying but must be accounted for in capacity predictions.
🔧
Residence Time Design LeversTo increase t_res: reduce N (lower screw speed), increase L (longer machine), reduce pitch P, or reduce S by accepting higher fill. Each lever has a trade-off — lower N reduces throughput; longer L increases capital cost; lower P may cause choking for cohesive materials. VECTRIX™ evaluates all these interactions simultaneously.

tdry=tconst + tfall

§ 9  Flow Regime Effects on Drying

Flow Regime Effects on Drying Uniformity

The bulk solid's flow regime — mass flow, funnel flow, or cohesive flow — significantly affects the residence time distribution (RTD) within the screw, which in turn determines the uniformity of drying across the product stream.

🟢
Mass Flow — Ideal for Drying
Narrow RTD. All material has similar residence time. Uniform moisture in the outlet. LMTD prediction is accurate. The dominant regime for well-designed jacketed screws with moderate fill.
🟡
Funnel Flow — Uneven Drying
Wide RTD. Some material bypasses while other material stagnates. Outlet moisture is non-uniform — some particles over-dried, some under-dried. Average outlet moisture may still meet target but with poor consistency.
🔴
Cohesive Flow — Poor Heat Transfer
Material plugs and slides intermittently. Reduced surface contact with the jacket wall. U_eff effectively drops. Residence time distribution is highly erratic. VFD and flow aids mandatory.
VECTRIX™ Treatment: The model applies a flow-regime correction to U_eff for non-mass-flow conditions: funnel flow → U_eff multiplied by 0.80; cohesive flow → U_eff multiplied by 0.65. These corrections are conservative estimates — actual values are material- and geometry-specific and should be verified by pilot testing for critical drying applications.
§ 10  Failure Modes

Failure Modes

💧Under-Drying

X_final > X_target. Insufficient residence time, heat duty, or LMTD. Product fails specification. Most common cause: speed too high, screw too short, or jacket temperature too low.

Fix: Reduce N, increase L, raise T_jacket, increase fill (↑ t_res), check fouling on jacket surface.

🔥Thermal Degradation

Material temperature exceeds temp_max. Sintering, color change, crystallization point, or fire risk. Over-dried and damaged product. Occurs when jacket T_h >> material T_max or when residence time is far too long at high temperature.

Fix: Check temp_max in material database. Reduce T_jacket. Shorten L or increase N to reduce t_res.

🧱Fouling & Scaling

Material deposits form on the jacket wall, progressively reducing U_eff. Particularly severe for high-sugar, high-starch, or sticky materials at elevated temperature. Can reduce heat duty by 30–60% over time.

Fix: Design for cleaning access (CIP). Use self-cleaning flights. Schedule periodic inspection intervals based on fouling rate estimate.

⚡Over-Drying — Energy Waste

X_final << X_target. Excess energy input, too slow screw, or jacket temperature too high. Product re-absorbs moisture on storage or becomes dusty. Energy consumption exceeds the thermodynamic minimum by a large margin.

Fix: Increase N or reduce L to reduce t_res. Lower T_jacket. VECTRIX™ flags motor oversizing and suggests optimization.

Introduction

Screw coolers are the standard solution when hot bulk solids must be cooled below 60–80°C before bagging, truck loading, or conveyor belt contact. They are preferable to rotary drum coolers where floor space is limited or where product containment is required (dusty, toxic, or odorous materials).

§ 3  NTU–Effectiveness Method

NTU–Effectiveness Method

The NTU–Effectiveness method calculates the heat exchanger performance from the number of transfer units (NTU) and the capacity ratio R. It avoids the need to know both inlet and outlet temperatures of both streams simultaneously — only the inlet conditions and the UA product are required.

3.1 — Number of Transfer Units
NTU=U · A / Cmin
UOverall heat transfer coefficient [W/m²K] — limited by the solid-side resistance; see §5
AEffective heat transfer area [m²] = π·D·L for full-wrap jacket. Additional area from hollow flights (if present) adds significantly — typically 1.5–3.0× the trough area.
CminSmaller heat capacity rate [W/K] — see §2.2
NTU rangeTypical screw coolers: NTU = 0.5–2.5. NTU < 0.5 → ineffective cooling. NTU > 3.0 → diminishing returns; increasing length no longer helps significantly.
3.2 — Theoretical Effectiveness (Counter-flow, C_min on solid side)
ε=(1 − e−NTU·(1−R)) / (1 − R·e−NTU·(1−R))
For R → 0 (isothermal coolant, e.g. large flow or condensing):  ε=1 − e−NTU
εTheoretical effectiveness [-] = (actual heat transferred) / (maximum possible heat transfer)
RCapacity ratio = C_min / C_max. For water cooling at high flow rates R ≈ 0 and the simplified formula applies. For air cooling R can approach 0.8–1.0.
3.3 — Actual Heat Transfer (Cooling Capacity)
Qactual=εactual·Cmin · (Tin − Tcool,in)
Tcool,inCooling medium inlet temperature [°C]. For water: typically 10–25°C. For air: ambient (15–35°C).
Tin − Tcool,inMaximum possible driving force — the larger this gap, the more cooling potential. Hot inlet material (T_in = 300°C) with cold water (15°C) gives 285°C driving force vs ambient air (35°C) giving only 265°C.

The NTU–Effectiveness formula assumes ideal counter-flow or parallel-flow. A screw cooler is neither — the bulk solid moves as a packed bed with partial rotation and axial mixing. This non-ideality reduces the effective performance below the theoretical maximum. A correction factor k_corr is applied.

§ 5  Heat Transfer Coefficient

Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient (U)

The solid-side resistance almost always dominates U in a screw cooler. The bulk solid is a poor conductor — heat must conduct through the packed material layer from the bulk to the wall. Unlike fluid heat exchangers, the wall resistance and coolant resistance are negligible by comparison.

5.1 — Resistance Model
1/U=1/hsolid + Rwall + 1/hcool
hsolidSolid-bed contact coefficient [W/m²K] — dominant resistance. Range: 30–120 W/m²K depending on material conductivity, particle size, and fill pressure.
RwallWall conduction resistance [m²K/W] ≈ t_wall / k_steel. For 6 mm steel: 6×10⁻³ / 50 = 1.2×10⁻⁴ m²K/W — negligible vs solid-side resistance.
hcoolCoolant-side coefficient [W/m²K]: water 3,000–10,000 (turbulent jacket) | air 20–80 (forced convection). Also negligible vs solid-side for water cooling.

Because h_solid dominates, design improvements should focus on maximising wall contact area (hollow flights, larger D) and increasing Cp of the solid (which increases C_min). Increasing coolant flow rate beyond the point where C_cool > C_solid provides diminishing benefit.

§ 8  Failure Modes

Failure Modes

🌡️Under-Cooling

T_out > T_target. Insufficient NTU, too high screw speed, or inadequate cooling medium flow. Product too hot for downstream handling, packaging, or storage — fire/explosion risk for some materials.

Fix: Reduce N, increase L, increase coolant flow, improve U (hollow flights), lower T_cool,in.

❄️Overcooling / Condensation

T_out below dew point or material minimum handling temperature. Moisture absorption, caking, or product damage. Particularly critical for hygroscopic materials.

Fix: Increase N, reduce coolant flow, raise T_cool,in, reduce jacket area.

🧱Fouling / Scale Build-up

Material deposits on jacket wall, reducing U over time. Worst for hot sticky materials (pitch, resins, sugars) or calcium-rich materials that precipitate at high surface temperatures.

Fix: Design A with 15–20% fouling allowance. Specify cleanable jacket connections. Consider flight scrapers.

💥Thermal Shock

Very rapid ΔT across the jacket wall can cause fracture in brittle materials (glass cullet, ceramics, sintered products) or severe residual stress. Also risks thermal fatigue in the screw shell itself.

Fix: Stage cooling in two passes. Use intermediate temperature cooling medium in first pass. Limit ΔT_wall to <100°C for brittle materials.

Screw Mixer Process Model

Engineering model for continuous screw mixers — integrating the Lacey mixing index, Axial Dispersion Model (ADM), Froude-based flow regime classification, and Newton number power model. § 1  Introduction

Introduction

🌀 Engineering Use Case Module Overview

Typical applications: Continuously blending two or more bulk solid components to achieve target blend uniformity — fertiliser NPK blending, animal feed formulation, cement additive dosing, food ingredient mixing.

Key industries:

Fertilisers & AgrochemicalsAnimal FeedFood IngredientsCement & ConcretePharmaceuticalsPlastics Compounding

Continuous screw mixers are chosen when throughputs are too high for batch mixers, when the blend ratio must be adjusted in real time, or when the mixing step must be integrated inline between upstream and downstream processes. A screw mixer homogenises two or more bulk solid components — or a single material with non-uniform properties — by repeatedly displacing and redistributing the particles across the screw cross-section during axial transport. Unlike batch mixers, a continuous screw mixer operates at a steady-state mixing quality determined by the balance between the axial dispersion (mixing) and the axial transport (plug flow).

Screw Separator Process Model

Engineering model for screw-based particle classification — using the Rosin-Rammler adapted sigmoid efficiency curve, velocity-shifted cut size, and residence time sharpness correction. Models the separation of polydisperse bulk solids by particle size class during screw conveying.

§ 1  Introduction

Introduction

🔬 Engineering Use Case Module Overview

Typical applications: Classifying or de-dusting bulk solids during conveying — removing fines from fertiliser pellets, scalping oversize from crushed minerals, separating fine powder from granular product.

Key industries:

FertilisersMining & AggregatesRecyclingFood ProcessingChemical Granulation

Screw separators are niche devices most suitable when a modest separation efficiency is acceptable and floor space or cost prohibits a dedicated screen or classifier. They are not suitable where sharp, high-efficiency separation is critical — in those cases, a vibrating screen or air classifier should be used after the screw.

Inline classificationNo additional equipmentAdjustable cut size (via speed)Gentle handling

A screw separator uses differences in particle size, density, or shape to segregate a mixed bulk solid stream into two or more fractions during conveying. The most common mechanism exploited in screw systems is size-based classification — finer particles tend to pass through gaps or screens in the trough floor while coarser particles are conveyed to the discharge end.

Industrial applications include: de-dusting of pelletised products (fertilisers, feed pellets), fines removal from granulated chemicals, scalping of oversize lumps from crushed minerals, and classification of recycled materials.

Unlike mechanical screens or air classifiers that operate on a stationary or suspended bed, the screw separator combines classification with continuous axial transport. The axial velocity of the bulk material directly influences separation performance — faster transport gives shorter contact time and degrades the sharpness of the size cut.

§ 2  Separation Principle and Cut Size

Separation Principle and Cut Size

Every separator is characterised by its cut size d₅₀ — the particle diameter at which 50% of the particles in that size class are separated (report to fines) and 50% are carried over (report to coarse product). Particles much smaller than d₅₀ are separated with high efficiency; particles much larger pass through with very low separation probability.

2.1 — Cut Size Definition
d50=particle diameter at which η = 0.50 [mm]
d50Nominal cut size at the reference axial velocity v_ref [mm]. This is the primary design input — specified by the process requirement (e.g. "remove all particles < 2 mm").
SharpnessA sharp separation (narrow transition from 0% to 100% efficiency) is desirable. Sharpness is quantified by σ — a smaller σ means a steeper efficiency curve around d₅₀.
2.2 — Velocity-Shifted Effective Cut Size
d50,eff=d50 · (vaxial / vref)0.3
vaxialActual axial transport velocity [m/s] = P · N / 60
vrefReference velocity at which d₅₀ was characterised [m/s] — typically 0.1–0.3 m/s for screw separators
0.3 exponentEmpirical velocity shift exponent. As axial velocity increases, the effective cut size rises — the separator cuts coarser. This models the reduced residence time available for fine particles to percolate through the bed and exit via the separation aperture. The 0.3 exponent reflects a sub-linear velocity sensitivity consistent with published classifier data.
Engineering implicationDoubling axial velocity increases d₅₀,eff by a factor of 2^0.3 ≈ 1.23 — a 23% coarsening of the effective cut size. Throughput vs. separation quality is a fundamental trade-off in screw separator design.

The grade efficiency curve — the plot of separation probability η vs. particle diameter d — is modelled as a sigmoid (logistic) function. This shape correctly represents the physical reality: very fine particles are separated with high probability; very coarse particles pass with very low probability; and there is a transition zone of finite width around d₅₀.

3.1 — Sigmoid Efficiency Function
η(d)=1 / (1 + e−k·(d − d50,eff)/σ)
ηGrade efficiency — probability that a particle of diameter d is separated (reports to fines product) [0–1]
dParticle diameter [mm]
d50,effVelocity-corrected cut size [mm] — from §2.2
σParticle size standard deviation of feed PSD [mm] — characterises the width of the transition zone. Narrow PSD (small σ) → sharper separation. Broad PSD (large σ) → diffuse grade efficiency curve.
k ≈ 1.5Empirical slope factor — controls the steepness of the sigmoid around d₅₀. Higher k → steeper cut (better separation sharpness). k = 1.5 is calibrated from industrial screw separator data.
Imperfection Factor: Real classifiers are never perfectly sharp. The ratio d₇₅/d₂₅ (the "imperfection" I) characterises the sharpness — I = 0 is a perfect step-function cut, I > 1 is poor classification. For screw separators, I typically ranges from 0.5–1.5 depending on geometry and operating conditions. The σ parameter in the sigmoid model maps directly to I: I ≈ 2σ/d₅₀.
3.2 — Overall Separation Efficiency
ηoverall=∫ η(d) · f(d) · dd
f(d)Particle size distribution of the feed [number or mass frequency]. The overall efficiency is the PSD-weighted average grade efficiency. A feed with most mass close to d₅₀ gives a lower overall efficiency than a feed where most mass is either well above or well below d₅₀.

Introduction

§ 2  Governing Reaction Model

First-Order Reaction Kinetics

The VECTRIX™ reactor model assumes first-order reaction kinetics — the reaction rate is proportional to the remaining concentration (or unconverted fraction) of the reactant. This is a well-validated approximation for many solid-phase thermal reactions where the rate is limited by either surface reaction or solid-state diffusion.

2.1 — First-Order Reaction Rate
dC/dt=−k · C
CReactant concentration or unconverted solid fraction [-]
kFirst-order reaction rate constant [1/s] — temperature-dependent via Arrhenius: k = A·exp(−E_a/RT)
EaActivation energy [kJ/mol] — characterises the temperature sensitivity of the reaction. High E_a reactions are very temperature-sensitive; small ΔT causes large Δk.
2.2 — Ideal Plug-Flow Conversion (upper bound)
XPFR=1 − e−k·tres=1 − e−Da
XFractional conversion [-] — fraction of reactant converted. X = 0.90 means 90% conversion.
DaDamköhler number = k · t_res [-] — see §3
⚠️ This is an upper boundThis equation assumes perfect plug flow. Real screw reactors have axial dispersion that reduces actual conversion below this value — see §4 for the correction.

§ 4  Axial Dispersion Correction

Axial Dispersion Model — The Critical Correction

A screw conveyor is NOT a plug-flow reactor. Some material moves faster than the mean (short residence time), some moves slower (long residence time). The Axial Dispersion Model (ADM) quantifies this spread and corrects the ideal plug-flow conversion to a more realistic value. For typical screw Pe values of 3–20, the uncorrected plug-flow prediction can overestimate conversion by 20–50%.

4.1 — Peclet Number
Pe=vaxial · L / Dax
DaxAxial dispersion coefficient [m²/s] — increases with shaft speed, fill fraction, and mixer element aggressiveness. For smooth screw flights: D_ax ≈ 0.001–0.01 m²/s. For paddle/plough elements: D_ax ≈ 0.005–0.05 m²/s.
4.2 — RTD Correction Factor for First-Order Reaction
RTDcorr=4α · ePe/2 / (1+α)² · eα·Pe/2 − (1−α)² · e−α·Pe/2
where α = √(1 + 4·Da/Pe)
RTDcorrDanckwerts closed-boundary solution for first-order reaction in a dispersed plug-flow reactor. This is the exact ADM result for first-order kinetics. Simplifies to 1.0 as Pe → ∞ and to the CSTR result as Pe → 0.
4.3 — Final Corrected Conversion
Xactual=(1 − e−Da) · RTDcorr
Rule of thumbAt Pe = 5 and Da = 2: X_PFR ≈ 0.865, X_actual ≈ 0.72 — a 17% reduction. At Pe = 10: X_actual ≈ 0.80. Only at Pe > 20 does X_actual approach X_PFR to within 5%.
Design implicationTo achieve 90% conversion accounting for axial dispersion, a screw reactor must be designed for X_PFR = 0.95–0.97 (Da = 3.0–3.5) rather than just X_PFR = 0.90 (Da = 2.3). This means 30–50% more residence time than the plug-flow model predicts.

§ 3  Pressure Model

Janssen-Style Back-Pressure Model

The back-pressure generated by the compacting plug resists the screw's forward thrust. The Janssen model — originally developed for grain silo pressure distribution — is adapted here for the compacting screw geometry. It accounts for the wall friction that partially transfers the axial stress to the trough wall, limiting the pressure build-up along the screw length.

3.1 — Axial Back-Pressure
Pback=Δρ · g · L · fwall
ΔρDensity increase = ρ_target − ρ_initial [kg/m³]. Higher density change → higher pressure required.
LCompaction zone length [m] — the length over which density increases from ρ_initial to ρ_target
fwallJanssen wall friction factor [-] ≈ 0.3–0.5. This represents the fraction of axial stress transferred to radial wall friction. Higher f_wall materials (cohesive, rough surface) build pressure faster over shorter lengths.
NoteThis is a simplified Janssen approximation. The full Janssen model includes a horizontal stress ratio K₀ = σ_h/σ_v and gives P = (ρ·g·A)/(K₀·μ·U) · (1 − e^(−K₀·μ·U·L/A)) where μ = wall friction coefficient, U = perimeter, A = cross-section. The simplified form is conservative and appropriate for preliminary design.
§ 4  Non-Linear Compaction Law

Non-Linear Compaction — Stress-Density Relationship

Bulk solid compaction is fundamentally non-linear — the first increment of density increase requires relatively little force, but subsequent increments require progressively more force as the void space is exhausted and particle-to-particle contact area increases. The power law model captures this behaviour.

4.1 — Compaction Stress-Density Power Law
σ=K · (ρ / ρ0)n
σApplied compressive stress [kPa] required to achieve density ρ
KMaterial compaction constant [kPa] — the stress required to double the density from ρ₀. Typical values: free-flowing powders K ≈ 5–20 kPa; granular minerals K ≈ 20–100 kPa; cohesive materials K ≈ 50–500 kPa.
nCompaction exponent [-] — typically 2–5 for bulk solids. Higher n means more non-linear (exponentially increasing force with density). n = 2: moderately non-linear (powders). n = 4–5: highly non-linear (granular minerals, tablets).
ρ0Reference (loose) density [kg/m³]

The non-linear behaviour explains why CR > 2.5 is a practical limit — the force required to increase density beyond this point grows exponentially and quickly exceeds the structural capacity of standard screw components.

Similarly, do not use λ_ref directly as λ — it is a 60% anchor, not the working resistance factor.

§ 8  Common Mistakes & Best Practices

Common Mistakes and Best Practices

❌Treating φ_max as actual fill

Overestimates capacity for inclined conveyors by up to 30%. Always use calcFill() output — not fill_max directly.

❌Ignoring cohesion in drive selection

Startup torque is underestimated by 40–100%. A shaft designed for running torque only can twist on first start with cohesive material.

⚠️Using dry-condition data for wet operation

Cohesion, flowability, and fill_max all change significantly with moisture. Always select or create the correct moisture-condition variant.

⚠️Wrong particle_class selection

Particle class governs both the λ calculation base value and the wear coefficient wc. A one-class error (e.g. B6 vs C1/2) changes λ by ~25% and wc by 2–3×.

§ 2  DIN — Deutsches Institut für Normung (German Institute for Standardization)

DIN Standards for Screw Conveyors

The DIN standards family covers European screw conveyor geometry, dimensional series, and performance calculation methods. DIN standards are widely adopted across Europe, the Middle East, and Asia-Pacific and define trough diameters, pitch ratios, shaft diameters, and component interoperability.

DIN 15261 — Screw Conveyors; Dimensions and Nominal Values

Defines the preferred nominal diameters and pitch series for screw conveyors. Establishes the relationship between trough diameter D and flight diameter, shaft diameter, and pitch p. VECTRIX™ geometry inputs are compatible with DIN 15261 nominal series — the standard diameter steps (100, 125, 160, 200, 250, 315, 400, 500, 630, 800 mm and above) align with the available geometry range in the platform.

DIN 15262 — Screw Conveyors; Design Rules and Calculation

Provides the European methodology for capacity and power calculation for screw conveyors, including the volume flow rate equation, fill degree limits, and peripheral speed recommendations. The DIN approach uses a resistance coefficient analogous to the VECTRIX™ λ parameter, and trough fill limits are consistent with the VECTRIX™ fill_max values stored per material class.

DIN 15263 — Screw Conveyors; Selection and Use of Screw Conveyors

Application guidance standard covering material classification for European practice, recommended drive arrangements, trough configurations (U-trough, tubular), and inclination limits. The inclination correction factors used in VECTRIX™ are consistent with the guidance ranges described in this standard.

DIN 15264 — Screw Conveyors; Troughs, End Plates, and Covers

Defines trough dimensional tolerances, end plate configurations, and cover arrangements for standard screw conveyors. Relevant to equipment procurement verification and dimensional cross-checking of VECTRIX™ geometry outputs.

§ 3  Mechanical Engineering Standards

Mechanical Component Standards

ISO 281 — Rolling Bearings — Dynamic Load Ratings and Rating Life

The international standard for rolling bearing life calculation. Defines the L10 life concept (90% reliability rating life in millions of revolutions) and the basic rating life equation relating applied load C_r, bearing dynamic capacity C, and life exponent p. Gearbox service factor selection in VECTRIX™ follows principles consistent with AGMA 2001 application service factor tables and ISO 6336 load spectrum concepts. ASME B106.1M — Design of Transmission Shafting

Provides the distortion energy (von Mises) approach to combined bending and torsion shaft design. VECTRIX™ implements the equivalent stress calculation for shaft design independently, using the combined stress formula equivalent to ASME B106.1M criteria. Shaft diameter sizing results can be cross-checked against this standard's recommended safety factors. See in the Mechanical tab.

IEC 60034 — Rotating Electrical Machines (Motor Standards)

Motor efficiency classes (IE1–IE4) and rated output/frame size standardization relevant to drive system selection. VECTRIX™ motor database entries are organized consistent with IEC 60034 frame and efficiency classifications.

§ 4  Process & Thermal Engineering References

Process and Thermal References

Incropera & DeWitt — Fundamentals of Heat and Mass Transfer

The primary heat transfer textbook reference for the VECTRIX™ thermal models. The NTU–Effectiveness method used in the Screw Cooler module and the LMTD approach used in the Screw Dryer module are standard heat exchanger analysis techniques described in this work. The formulation of overall heat transfer coefficient U and the log-mean temperature difference ΔTlm are standard results from classical thermodynamics, not proprietary to any specific publication.

Mujumdar — Handbook of Industrial Drying

A widely used reference for drying kinetics. The two-period drying model (constant-rate period followed by falling-rate period) and the concept of critical moisture content Xcr as the transition point between periods are classical results described in this and similar handbooks. VECTRIX™ implements the dual-period drying model independently using the standard drying rate equations.

Levenspiel — Chemical Reaction Engineering

Foundation reference for the Screw Reactor module. The plug-flow reactor (PFR) first-order conversion formula, the Damköhler number (Da), and the Peclet number axial dispersion correction (Danckwerts boundary condition) are classical results from Levenspiel's treatment of tubular reactors with dispersion. § 5  Bulk Solids Engineering References

Bulk Solids and Flowability References

Jenike — Storage and Flow of Solids (1964)

The foundational work in bulk solids flow analysis. Jenike introduced the flow function, the flowability index ffc (ratio of consolidation stress to unconfined yield strength), and the critical outlet dimension concept for hopper design. VECTRIX™ uses Jenike flowability terminology in the material database — the flowability field (1–4) is calibrated to correspond to Jenike's qualitative categories (free-flowing → cohesive → very cohesive/non-flowing). The numeric values are VECTRIX™'s independent discretization, not a direct reproduction of Jenike's flow function curves.

Rabinowicz — Friction and Wear of Materials

Reference for the Archard-type wear model used in VECTRIX™ liner life estimation. The proportionality between wear volume, applied load, sliding distance, and material hardness ratio (the Archard wear equation) is a classical result described in Rabinowicz and in Archard's original 1953 paper. VECTRIX™ implements a simplified version of this relationship through the wear coefficient wc, which is derived from the material's abrasion class and particle size — not from any proprietary wear test database.

Rhodes — Introduction to Particle Technology

Reference for particle size classification conventions and bulk solid behavioural characterization used in the VECTRIX™ material model, including the angle of repose, bulk density variation with consolidation state, and particle size distribution effects on flowability.

§ 14  Common Input Mistakes & Troubleshooting

Common Mistakes & How to Fix Them

❌Wrong material selected

Symptom: Power wildly over- or under-estimated. Fix: Check ρ, λ_ref, and flowability match your actual material. Create a custom entry with measured values.

❌Capacity target in wrong units

Symptom: Screw massively over-sized. Fix: Confirm t/h vs m³/h — the calculator accepts mass flow. Divide volumetric flow by bulk density to get mass flow.

⚠️Using fill_max as actual fill

Symptom: Capacity overestimate for inclined conveyors. Fix: φ is always computed by calcFill() — never use fill_max directly. See §3 Stored vs. Computed in Section 12.

⚠️Ignoring startup torque

Symptom: Motor trips on start, shaft coupling fails. Fix: Always check Tstart/Trun ratio in the Drive tab. For cohesive materials this can be 3–5×. Add VFD or soft-starter.

⚠️Moisture condition mismatch

Symptom: Dryer or cooler underperforms in plant. Fix: Select the wet/moist material variant, not the dry entry. Cohesion and λ change significantly with moisture.

⚠️Process geometry not from §05

Symptom: Process module and conveyor designs are inconsistent. Fix: Always carry D, L, P, N from a completed Screw Conveyor design into process modules. Do not invent geometry independently.

💡Pitch not matched to duty

Symptom: Excessive power for free-flowing material, or material slip-back on inclines. Fix: Standard pitch = D. Use short pitch (0.67D) for sticky/inclined; long pitch (1.5D) for fast-moving free-flowing. Adjust in Core Design.

💡Critical speed not checked

Symptom: Excessive vibration in plant. Fix: Always check the Mechanical tab after finalising N. Operating speed must be < 0.8 × Nc. Long screws have low Nc — add hanger bearings to raise it.
